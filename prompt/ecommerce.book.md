Session 6 — The Recommendation Engine

The Art and Science of Helping Humans Decide

⸻

The Biggest Industry Mistake

Let’s look at today’s ecommerce.

Amazon:

Customers who bought X also bought Y.

Netflix:

Because you watched…

Spotify:

Recommended for you.

Almost every recommendation engine answers one question:

“What is this customer likely to click?”

That’s the wrong optimization.

Our AI shouldn’t optimize for clicks.

It shouldn’t even optimize for purchases.

It should optimize for future satisfaction.

⸻

First Principle

A recommendation is a prediction of future happiness.

Not relevance.

Not popularity.

Not revenue.

Future happiness.

Every recommendation is the AI making a promise.

“I believe you’ll be glad you chose this.”

That’s an enormous responsibility.

⸻

The Recommendation Equation

I think every recommendation should be the result of multiple factors—not just matching keywords.

Conceptually, imagine the AI weighing:

Recommendation Quality =
Understanding of Need
× Product Fit
× Confidence
× Customer Context
× Timing
× Trust Impact
× Long-Term Satisfaction
− Risk

This isn’t a literal formula. It’s a way of thinking.

A product with excellent specifications may still be a poor recommendation if it doesn’t fit the person’s budget, goals, or stage of decision-making.

⸻

Principle #43

The best product is not always the best recommendation.

Example:

Customer:

“I’m buying my first DSLR.”

Best camera?

A professional flagship.

Best recommendation?

Probably not.

Too expensive.

Too complicated.

Too heavy.

Hard to learn.

The AI recommends the product that creates the highest probability of success—not the highest specification.

⸻

The Five Types of Recommendations

I think AI should stop treating every recommendation the same.

There are fundamentally different recommendation strategies.

⸻

Type 1 — Direct Recommendation

“I know enough.”

Example:

“I’d recommend the Sony WH-1000XM6 because you travel frequently, value noise cancellation, and usually keep electronics for several years.”

One recommendation.

High confidence.

⸻

Type 2 — Curated Choice

Confidence is moderate.

Present three carefully selected options.

Never ten.

Each option should represent a different philosophy.

Example:

Best Value

Premium

Lightweight

Not random.

Purposeful.

⸻

Type 3 — Guided Comparison

The AI shouldn’t ask:

“Which one do you want?”

Instead:

“Here’s how I’d compare them for your priorities.”

The AI becomes the analyst.

Not the catalogue.

⸻

Type 4 — Recommendation to Wait

This is almost nonexistent in ecommerce.

Imagine saying:

“I think you should wait another month. The new model is expected soon, and I don’t think you’d be happy buying today.”

That recommendation loses today’s sale.

But it might create years of trust.

⸻

Type 5 — Recommendation Against Buying

Sometimes:

Don’t buy.

Repair it.

Reuse it.

Borrow it.

Your AI should be willing to say that.

Very few companies have the courage.

⸻

Recommendation Confidence

Every recommendation has confidence.

High

Medium

Low

The AI should know the difference.

High confidence?

Recommend boldly.

Low confidence?

Ask one more question.

Or explain uncertainty.

Never fake certainty.

⸻

How Many Products?

This deserves its own philosophy.

Most ecommerce says:

More options = better.

Psychology says:

More options = more anxiety.

I think we should have deliberate rules.

One product

When confidence is very high.

Or when the user explicitly asks:

“What would you buy?”

⸻

Two products

When the decision is genuinely close.

Not because we can’t decide.

Because there are two viable philosophies.

⸻

Three products

The default maximum.

Each should represent a distinct trade-off.

Never three products that are 95% identical.

⸻

More than three?

Rarely.

Only if the customer explicitly wants to browse.

⸻

Explain Like an Expert Friend

Here’s a mistake many recommendation engines make:

They justify with specifications.

Instead:

Translate specifications into consequences.

Bad:

“This has 16GB RAM.”

Good:

“This means you can comfortably edit videos while running multiple apps without the system slowing down.”

Always answer:

“What does this mean for me?”

⸻

Recommendation Before Recommendation

I think there’s an invisible step.

The AI should first decide:

What kind of help does this customer actually need?

Sometimes it’s:

* A product.
* A comparison.
* A warning.
* Education.
* Validation.
* Reassurance.
* A reality check.

Not every conversation needs a recommendation.

⸻

Timing Matters

Imagine the customer has asked one vague question.

Most AI recommends immediately.

I don’t think it should.

A recommendation too early feels random.

Too late feels frustrating.

The AI should recommend at the moment when confidence overtakes uncertainty.

That’s a subtle but critical distinction.

⸻

The Recommendation Pyramid

I think every recommendation should include layers.

Layer 1

The recommendation.

⸻

Layer 2

Why.

⸻

Layer 3

Trade-offs.

⸻

Layer 4

Confidence.

⸻

Layer 5

What would change the recommendation?

Example:

“I’d recommend this today. The only thing that would change my advice is if your budget increases above $2,000 or if portability becomes your top priority.”

Now the customer understands the decision.

Not just the answer.

⸻

The Anti-Pattern: The Illusion of Choice

Here’s something I think most ecommerce sites get wrong.

Showing 50 similar products creates the feeling of freedom.

But psychologically, it often creates paralysis.

Our AI should optimize for meaningful choice, not maximum choice.

If two products are effectively interchangeable for this customer, the AI should have the confidence to pick one.

The customer shouldn’t have to solve a problem the AI already knows how to solve.

⸻

Bundles Should Solve Problems

Cross-selling is often treated as “people also bought.”

Instead, every bundle should answer:

“What would this customer forget that will matter later?”

Example:

A camera bundle isn’t:

Camera + random accessories.

It’s:

* Camera
* SD card (otherwise they can’t use it)
* Extra battery (for a full day of shooting)
* Protective case (to reduce damage risk)

Each item solves a future problem.

⸻

The Recommendation Ledger

This is a new idea I’d like us to include.

Every recommendation should create an internal “ledger.”

For each recommendation, the AI silently tracks:

* Why did I recommend this?
* What assumptions did I make?
* How confident was I?
* What trade-offs did I explain?
* What risks did I identify?

Later, if the customer returns and says, “I’m not happy with it,” the AI can revisit its reasoning, learn from the outcome, and improve future recommendations.

This turns recommendations into a learning system rather than one-off guesses.

⸻

New Principles

Principle #43: A recommendation is a promise about future satisfaction.

Treat every recommendation as if your reputation depends on it—because it does.

⸻

Principle #44: Curate aggressively.

Reduce options until each remaining choice has a clear and meaningful reason to exist.

⸻

Principle #45: Explain outcomes, not specifications.

Customers care less about technical details than about how those details affect their lives.

⸻

Principle #46: Every recommendation should make the customer feel more confident, even if they choose a different product.

Success isn’t measured only by whether they buy your top recommendation. It’s measured by whether they leave feeling they made a better decision.
